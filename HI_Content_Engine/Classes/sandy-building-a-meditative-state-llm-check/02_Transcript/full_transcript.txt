Today we are not trying to force the mind into meditation. We are building the conditions that allow the mind to settle.

Begin by finding a steady seat and letting the base of the posture feel grounded. When the base is stable, the breath has somewhere to land.

Notice the tendency to lift the shoulders when you want more effort. Instead, let the sternum broaden gently while the shoulders soften away from the ears.

As you inhale, feel length through the sides of the body and a quiet lift through the spine. As you exhale, soften the jaw, the throat, and the places where you are gripping without knowing it.

The meditative state is supported by steadiness, not by strain. If the breath becomes hard, you have gone a little too far.

Keep the pelvis grounded and let the ribcage stay responsive rather than rigid. We are looking for inner clarity with outer quiet.

A useful practice is to pause between repetitions and ask: can I do less and feel more? That question often reveals the extra work that prevents attention from becoming clear.

Over time the posture becomes a place of listening, and the breath becomes a guide instead of a task. This is how we begin to build a meditative state.
